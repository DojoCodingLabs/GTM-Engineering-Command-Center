# **Part I. Executive Summary: The State of Google Ads in 2026**

Google Ads in 2026 is no longer a keyword management discipline. It is a signal architecture discipline. Across all four research passes folded into this Atlas, the same conclusion surfaces from different angles: the gap between an average advertiser and a sophisticated operator is not campaign setup, bid tweaking, or even creative. It is the quality of the conversion truth fed back into the machine. Google itself now tells advertisers that hyper-granular legacy Search structures can be a hidden cost, while the practitioner community has converged on consolidated structures, broad match paired with Smart Bidding, and an obsessive focus on offline conversion imports, Enhanced Conversions, Customer Match, and value-based bidding.

For info products and digital SaaS specifically, this matters even more than for e-commerce, because the revenue event sits several stages downstream of the click. A webinar registration, a free-trial start, or a cheap form fill is not a sale. If you optimize to those proxy events without sending the real outcome back, Google will find you an endless supply of them, and almost none will turn into revenue. The operators winning in 2026 are not the ones with the fanciest automation stack. They are the ones with the cleanest truth signals, the sharpest temperature-aware offer design, and the discipline to reject platform-reported wins that do not survive CRM or incrementality scrutiny.

## **The Five Laws of 2026 Google Ads**

| Law 1\. Signal beats structure. The account that feeds the cleanest conversion truth wins. Offline conversion imports, Enhanced Conversions for Leads, and value-based bidding are the operating system now, not a reporting afterthought. Manual bid tinkering is a rounding error next to data quality. |
| :---- |

| Law 2\. Isolate brand or lie to yourself. Mixed brand and non-brand traffic inflates reported ROAS and mis-teaches the bidding system about real-market demand. A dedicated brand campaign plus brand exclusions on every non-brand and automated campaign is non-negotiable. PMax can backfill 10 to 42 percent of its reported conversions from your own brand if you let it. |
| :---- |

| Law 3\. Performance Max for lead gen is guilty until proven innocent. Left alone, PMax finds the cheapest, lowest-quality lead it possibly can. Only CRM-stage feedback, qualified-lead or sale-stage optimization, brand exclusions, audience inputs, geo filters, and schedule controls make it honest. Treat low CPLs as a hypothesis to be tested against pipeline, never as a result. |
| :---- |

| Law 4\. Browse surfaces shape demand; Search captures it. Treat Demand Gen and YouTube like paid social, not like watered-down Search. They need creative-led audience selection and lower-friction offers (quizzes, cheatsheets, short VSLs, webinar clips). Forcing cold browse traffic into a hard demo or application ask produces weak quality or policy trouble. |
| :---- |

| Law 5\. Automate selectively, and never on a rep's say-so. Adopt Smart Bidding, AI Max, and PMax only after signal hygiene is in place. Keep Max Clicks, exact match, or manual steering alive longer than a Google rep would prefer if the account does not yet have the data integrity to support full automation. Use reps for product availability and implementation details, not for budget allocation or channel mix. |
| :---- |

# **Part II. Account Architecture and Campaign Structure**

The consolidation of Google's matching algorithms and real-time Smart Bidding has rewritten the structural rules of paid acquisition. The hyper-granular accounts of the last decade fragmented conversion signals across sparse ad groups; modern structure concentrates data so the machine learning has enough density to optimize.

## **The death of SKAGs and the rise of Hagakure**

The debate between Single Keyword Ad Groups and consolidated, thematic structures has reached a clear consensus: roughly nine of ten performance marketers now favor consolidated architectures. A persistent minority (for instance the agency Disco Sloth) still defends SKAGs for accounts that need strict Quality Score control, precise ad-copy alignment, and manual CPC, and that position remains defensible for small, low-data accounts where chance dominates outcomes. But for any account on Smart Bidding, exact is no longer exact, broad is becoming the default, and granularity now starves the algorithm rather than controlling it.

The Hagakure method, popularized through Think with Google, organizes ad groups around landing-page themes rather than individual keywords, uses broad and phrase match, supplies many Responsive Search Ad assets, and adds a Dynamic Search Ad group to catch low-volume URLs. A common operational rule: one URL per ad group, only URLs above roughly 3,000 impressions per week earn their own ad group, and everything else consolidates. Jyll Saskin Gales frames the practical version succinctly: start consolidated, and only split when goal, budget, bid strategy, geography, or materially different audience intent genuinely justify it.

**WHITEHAT  |  10/10**    *Thematic Hagakure grouping uses Google's native consolidation to concentrate conversion data without manipulating queries.*

**GRAYHAT  |  4/10**    *Stuffing exact, phrase, and broad duplicates into one campaign for coverage rarely helps, creates budget cannibalization, and muddies reporting.*

## **Brand versus non-brand isolation**

Mixing brand and non-brand traffic in one campaign lets the bidding algorithm chase cheap branded queries to satisfy a blended tROAS or tCPA, inflating reported performance, hiding weak non-brand terms, and burning budget on traffic that would have converted organically. The fix is a dedicated brand campaign, often on manual CPC with strict max-bid caps so Google cannot charge premium auction rates for your own name, plus brand exclusions applied to every non-brand and automated campaign. In practice a tight brand campaign can run around 1,200 percent ROAS on roughly 5 to 7 percent of budget; that is exactly why it must be reported separately rather than smeared across the account.

**WHITEHAT  |  10/10**    *Pillar-based brand separation produces transparent reporting and budget control under standard guidelines.*

## **The intent ladder: four to five lanes**

The best info-product and SaaS Search architectures isolate distinct intent lanes so that incompatible journeys are not force-fit into a single bid target. Build at minimum four lanes, and a fifth for SaaS:

* **Brand.** Defends your own name at high ROAS and low CPC; isolated from everything else.

* **Non-brand high intent.** Outcome, mechanism, and evaluation queries with real purchase intent.

* **Broad discovery and expansion.** Broad match plus Smart Bidding to find queries manual research misses, fenced by aggressive negatives.

* **Competitor and conquesting.** Comparison and alternative queries in their own campaign, never sharing budget with core non-brand.

* **SaaS only: company-size or use-case clusters.** SMB self-serve demo queries behave nothing like enterprise RFP-style queries and should bid separately.

For info products the analog is an intent ladder of pain-aware, mechanism-aware, competitor, and outcome clusters, rather than a single buy-now campaign. This is not officially prescribed; it is how experienced operators reconcile thematic consolidation with real funnel differences.

## **The five-pillar funnel orchestration matrix**

A practical budget allocation that recurs across the structured research, balancing immediate capture against demand creation:

| Pillar | Budget share | Bidding and match |
| :---- | :---- | :---- |
| 1\. Brand protection | 5 to 7 percent | Manual CPC, exact match |
| 2\. High-intent product | 50 to 60 percent | tCPA, phrase and broad match |
| 3\. Competitor conquest | 15 to 20 percent | tCPA, exact and phrase match |
| 4\. Problem-aware | 10 to 15 percent | Maximize Conversions, broad match |
| 5\. Remarketing and nurture | 10 percent | Maximize Conversions, audience lists |

Note that this is a starting allocation for a steady-state account. The phased version, where early budgets concentrate almost entirely on bottom-of-funnel capture before expanding into demand creation, appears in Part XIV.

# **Part III. Performance Max Control and the Moran Feeder Strategy**

Performance Max offers broad inventory access but is routinely criticized for opacity and lack of search control. The 2025 to 2026 reality is that Google handed back meaningful levers, and the real craft is using them to wrestle control back from the automation.

## **The control levers that now exist**

As of 2026 the PMax control surface includes: channel-level reporting (so you can finally see Search versus YouTube versus Display spend), full search-terms insights, campaign-level negative keyword lists raised from 100 to 10,000 in March 2025, account-level negatives capped at 1,000, brand exclusions, customer-list exclusions that are real exclusions rather than mere signals, demographic and device controls, and budget pacing reports.

* **Brand exclusions versus negatives versus restrictions.** Use entity-based Brand Exclusions to strip Search and Shopping brand traffic (they catch misspellings, variants, and other languages). Use negative keyword lists when you also need to exclude Video or Display brand traffic. Use Brand Restrictions to build a brand-only PMax. Reserve account-level negatives for universal junk such as free, jobs, salary, login, and careers.

* **Feed-only or limited-asset setups.** For physical tripwire goods, a feed-only build (no headlines, descriptions, images, or video; Final URL Expansion off; Automatically Created Assets off) forces Shopping-like behavior onto high-intent placements. Caveat: Merchant Center typically allows only one such asset group; adding more in Google Ads triggers the three-headline minimum that breaks feed-only.

* **Asset-group-as-persona.** Treat each asset group like a paid-social ad set, one persona with its own audience signal, search themes, and creative. But Optmyzr's data complicates this: multiple campaigns with a single asset group often outperformed more cluttered persona structures, and for lead gen there is no Listing Groups tab, so segment by campaign instead. Segment only when personas truly need different creative and URLs.

**WHITEHAT  |  9/10**    *Feed-only and limited-asset setups use Google's native asset rules to restrict placements; fully within the allowed configuration.*

**WHITEHAT  |  9/10**    *Aggressive brand exclusions and negatives keep automated campaigns from backfilling easy brand demand.*

**WHITEHAT  |  8/10**    *Persona asset groups help when segmentation matches real demand and hurt when they just fragment data.*

## **The lead-gen spam problem and how to kill it**

PMax for lead gen gets spammed heavily, and the primary source is its reach on YouTube and mobile app inventory, flagged by referral parameters such as mobile.yt or mobile.youtube.com in click logs. Search Engine Land's Laura Schiele is blunt that PMax is simultaneously great at volume and great at finding the lowest-quality leads it possibly can when left to optimize cheap events. The fixes that work:

* **Optimize to a deeper, human-only action.** Bid to qualified lead, purchase, or webinar-attended via Enhanced Conversions and offline import, never the raw form fill. This is the single most consistent anti-spam lever in PMax and Search.

* **Strip video assets.** Removing video from a lead-gen PMax avoids most YouTube and mobile.yt spam. This is signal manipulation, technically compliant but deliberate.

* **Harden the form.** Replace single-field email captures with multi-step qualification (company size, current stack, specific pain) so bot auto-fill templates fail. Layer reCAPTCHA v3 and hidden honeypot fields that filter bot submissions before the conversion fires.

* **Cap custom conversions early and prune placements.** Keep to one or two custom conversions in the learning window; review PMax placement reports and exclude low-value mobile app inventory.

**WHITEHAT  |  10/10**    *Honeypots and reCAPTCHA are standard security to filter fraudulent bot activity.*

**GRAYHAT  |  5/10**    *Removing video specifically to game spam routing is intentional signal manipulation, allowed but not what Google intends.*

## **The John Moran feeder strategy**

The feeder strategy, associated with John Moran of Solutions 8 and Tier 11, is one of the most discussed beyond-the-docs playbooks. In its e-commerce form, a Standard Shopping campaign on a low tROAS (around 400 percent) or Maximize Clicks acts as the feeder, capturing cold high-intent traffic and populating first-party remarketing pools; a feed-only PMax on a higher tROAS (around 500 percent) then converts those warmed audiences. In the lead-gen adaptation, a low-friction high-intent Search campaign is the feeder and a no-video PMax remarketing campaign is the converter, fed only by verified leads. On Perpetual Traffic, Moran also detailed using deliberately higher tROAS targets to push ad rank, and therefore CPC, down on purpose, forcing cheaper clicks while PMax acts as the second-click conversion layer.

| Honesty caveat on the feeder numbers The widely cited results are e-commerce, not lead gen. A GrowMyAds case showed revenue up 108 percent (about $37,000 added monthly, from a $50k baseline to over $100k) on only 54 percent more spend, conversions up 106 percent, and ROAS climbing from 5.8x to nearly 8.0x in 22 days. Another single-product test on the Google Ads Aficionados podcast produced a 127.81 percent revenue increase. These are Shopping plus PMax e-commerce cases. The lead-gen spam-reduction benefit is described qualitatively by Moran and Perpetual Traffic, not cleanly quantified in public, and one circulating lead-gen figure (around 215 percent more spend with an 84 percent CPA drop and 63 percent higher ROAS) is loosely sourced. Treat the feeder as a strong, real structure; treat the headline percentages as e-commerce evidence. |
| :---- |

**GRAYHAT  |  7/10**    *The deliberate push-ad-rank-down maneuver exploits auction mechanics in an unintended way; no policy violation and sustainable. The coordination of Shopping and PMax bidding itself is whitehat.*

## **What the Optmyzr data actually shows**

Optmyzr's 9,199-account PMax study is the most useful neutral evidence on where real advertisers land. Eighty-two percent ran PMax alongside other campaign types; multiple campaigns with a single asset group tended to show the best ROAS; accounts under roughly 60 conversions performed worse across most metrics; and video is the closest thing to a PMax magic button, because text-only success is largely confined to Search. Kirk Williams of ZATO adds the consistent practitioner read: smaller accounts struggle with PMax, while larger, data-rich accounts do well.

# **Part IV. Smart Bidding and Signal Engineering**

Modern Search lives and dies on automated bidding, and the craft is managing the signals you feed it rather than the bids themselves.

## **The migration ladder**

The settled doctrine for moving a campaign up the automation stack:

* Manual CPC at initial launch, to seed core match and click signals.

* Maximize Clicks briefly, to build traffic and base data (get off this as fast as possible).

* Maximize Conversions, then Target CPA once volume reaches roughly 30 conversions per month per campaign.

* Maximize Conversion Value, then Target ROAS once you have about 60 days of value data and at least two distinct conversion values.

Below roughly 30 conversions per month per campaign, do not use tROAS or aggressive broad match; consolidate instead. Navah Hopkins argues that accounts under about $5,000 per month often should not be on Smart Bidding at all yet, and the community feeder logic echoes this.

## **The 70 to 80 percent target rule**

Setting aggressive automated targets too early throttles delivery. The widely used guardrail (associated with agency practice such as Tegra) is to set the initial tCPA or tROAS at 70 to 80 percent of trailing actual performance, give it about two weeks to stabilize, then tighten in roughly 10 percent increments. Set a tROAS at the profitable floor, not the historical average.

**WHITEHAT  |  10/10**    *A standard pacing tactic to prevent algorithmic throttling.*

## **Value-based bidding for low-volume and high-ticket**

High-ticket info products (five-figure coaching cohorts) and enterprise SaaS often produce fewer than 10 closed deals per month, which is far below the volume Smart Bidding needs. The answer is a tiered micro-conversion value ladder, assigning calculated values to upstream actions so the campaign maintains the conversion density to bid on, while still steering toward real revenue:

| Funnel stage | Illustrative assigned value |
| :---- | :---- |
| Free trial activated | $50 |
| Product demo requested | $500 |
| Qualified lead or SQL | $900 to $2,000 |
| Sales opportunity created | $3,000 |
| Closed-won revenue | Actual value, e.g. $15,000 |

Without tiered values, the algorithm treats a $5k trial and a $500k enterprise opportunity as equally valuable. You need at least two unique conversion values and several complete conversion cycles before tROAS is stable. Firebrand's warning is worth heeding: offline conversion tracking plus value bidding can quietly wreck a campaign if the click-to-deal lag exceeds the bidding lookback or you over-optimize to a rare deep event. Keep the data flowing for insight even when you do not let it drive bidding.

**WHITEHAT  |  10/10**    *Feeding factual revenue and stage values back to Google is best practice and the most consistent anti-spam lever.*

**GRAYHAT  |  4/10**    *Assigning uniform static values to leads of genuinely different quality skews returns; manipulating reported values purely to game bidding distorts signals and risks policy issues if deceptive.*

**BLACKHAT  |  2/10**    *Generating false conversion events (bot clicks, fake test leads) to inflate volume and manipulate Smart Bidding violates policy and can trigger suspension.*

## **The 2026 AI bidding stack**

Google is pushing the bidding layer hard. Smart Bidding Exploration is positioned as the biggest bidding update in over a decade, and Google claims Search campaigns using it have seen 27 percent more unique converting users (a Google figure, treat as marketing). By Google Marketing Live 2026 that logic had expanded toward Performance Max and Shopping. Journey-aware bidding is explicitly designed to learn from the full customer journey rather than only the last biddable conversion, which is potentially meaningful for long-cycle SaaS and high-ticket info products, though independent public evidence remains thin.

# **Part V. Conversion Tracking and Privacy-First Signal Infrastructure**

With third-party cookies deprecating across major browsers in 2026, tracking has to rest on first-party, consent-aware, server-side infrastructure. This is the single highest-return work in the entire Atlas and the prerequisite for every advanced tactic in it.

## **Enhanced Conversions, web and for leads**

Enhanced Conversions for Leads hashes the lead email with SHA-256 and matches it to signed-in Google users even when the GCLID expired or was never captured. Target match rate for B2B SaaS is roughly 40 to 65 percent; below 30 percent usually signals field-mapping problems. This is what lets a closed-won deal months later still credit the click that started it.

## **Consent Mode v2 mechanics**

Consent Mode v2 has been mandatory for EEA and UK traffic since March 2024\. It governs four parameters: ad\_storage (advertising cookies), analytics\_storage (site analytics), ad\_user\_data (whether user data goes to Google for ads), and ad\_personalization (whether remarketing and personalized lists populate). In Advanced mode, when a user denies tracking, the tags do not read or write advertising cookies; they send cookieless pings (timestamp, user agent, ad-click context) that Google's models use to reconstruct lost conversions. Modeling needs roughly 700 ad clicks over seven days per country and domain to engage.

| The recovery numbers, stated honestly Google's headline up to 70 percent recovery is a ceiling for high-volume properties, not a default. The more reliable figures: a median around 17 percent conversion lift from modeling alone, and 30 to 50 percent recovery when you stack Enhanced Conversions plus Advanced Consent Mode plus server-side tagging. Server-side tagging (sGTM), run through a private subdomain such as tracking.yourbrand.com, also bypasses Safari Intelligent Tracking Prevention and browser ad blockers, forwarding the gcs and gcd consent strings to the server before dispatching hashed payloads via the Conversions API. |
| :---- |

**WHITEHAT  |  10/10**    *Server-side tracking with explicit consent verification and Advanced Consent Mode respect user choice under GDPR and the DMA.*

**GRAYHAT  |  6/10**    *Cross-domain cookie syncing to build audiences across unaffiliated sites can bypass modern browser privacy controls.*

**BLACKHAT  |  1/10**    *Consent-faking scripts that pass granted parameters when a user opted out violate privacy law and policy and invite immediate suspension.*

## **The CRM offline conversion import loop**

For long-cycle SaaS and high-ticket funnels, web-tag tracking cannot connect a click today to a closed-won sale months later. The industry standard is the GCLID-to-CRM loop: capture the GCLID in a hidden form field, save it to the CRM lead, and as the deal moves through MQL, SQL, opportunity, and closed-won, upload those milestones back to Google. Because GCLIDs expire after 90 days, expand attribution windows to at least a 30-day click and 90-day conversion window to capture the cycle. For a demo-led SaaS motion, import stage values such as MQL around $10 to $50, SQL around $100 to $500, and actual deal value for closed-won, which matches how mature HubSpot and Salesforce integrations are implemented in the field.

Independent and vendor data both point the same way. Involve Digital reports that B2B SaaS accounts running offline conversion tracking generate about 3x more pipeline at roughly 31 percent lower cost per lead. GrowthSpree reports attribution coverage moving from 25 to 40 percent on default setups to 85 to 95 percent with full implementation within 30 days, and SQL volume up 30 to 50 percent at the same spend by day 90\. Both are self-reported; treat as directional, but the direction is unambiguous.

**WHITEHAT  |  10/10**    *Importing real revenue and pipeline outcomes is the operational core of value-based bidding and the foundation of a defensible account.*

# **Part VI. Keywords and Match Types in the Broad-Match Era**

Google has shifted targeting from exact keyword matching to semantic intent matching. In 2026 the question is not which match type you prefer; it is whether your signals and negatives are strong enough to let broad match work.

## **When broad match wins, and when it bleeds you**

In accounts with clean conversion tracking, first-party signals, and value-based bidding, broad match consistently beats phrase and exact by surfacing relevant queries manual research misses. In accounts with poor tracking or thin budgets, broad match is the fastest way to exhaust a budget on low-intent traffic. Optmyzr's 2026 Match Type Study of 30,000 accounts found exact has shed nearly ten percentage points of spend share since 2022 while broad has become the dominant match type by budget, and phrase leads on conversion rate in both e-commerce and lead-gen segments. Jyll Saskin Gales' 2026 stance is unusually explicit: she increasingly prefers exact or broad, often in separate campaigns, because broad otherwise hogs shared budget and prevents exact from getting real delivery, and she no longer treats phrase as the dependable Goldilocks option.

Sarah Stemen cites 30 to 50 conversions as the practical threshold before broad match has enough truth data to behave, and Optmyzr found most successful PMax accounts had crossed 60 or more conversions.

**WHITEHAT  |  9/10**    *Splitting broad and exact when budgets are tight keeps broad from starving exact and lets budgets express intent.*

## **The search-terms report is degrading, so mine n-grams**

Google's own help documentation states the search-terms report only includes terms used by a significant number of people, which is precisely why practitioners still rely on n-gram analysis and custom search-term mining to find waste patterns that never surface cleanly in the UI. The degradation bites harder in broad-match and AI-assisted accounts than in old exact-match builds.

GrowthSpree's B2B Ads Waste report, auditing 43 enterprise accounts representing $31.2 million in spend, found an average of 36.1 percent of spend (about $11.3 million) wasted on non-converting search terms, driven mostly by unmonitored broad-match keywords. One generic broad keyword converted at 0.009 percent across nearly 63,000 clicks; a single unmonitored broad keyword burned $187,000 over twelve months. The defense is a weekly (daily on high spend) n-gram review that aggregates clicks, cost, and conversions across recurring one, two, and three-word patterns, then builds negatives from performance data rather than guesswork.

**WHITEHAT  |  10/10**    *N-gram waste aggregation analyzes your own account data to eliminate wasted spend.*

## **Competitor conquesting: ethics and execution**

Bidding on competitor terms is standard for SaaS and info products, but bare brand names (for instance HubSpot) draw high CPCs and low conversion because most searchers want a login. Target comparison intent instead: alternatives, pricing, versus queries. To stay compliant, never use Dynamic Keyword Insertion in competitor campaigns, since auto-inserting a trademarked name into a headline triggers immediate policy violations, and send traffic to objective, sourced comparison pages rather than generic landers.

**WHITEHAT  |  9/10**    *Comparison and alternative bidding is permissible under fair-use guidelines when trademarks stay out of ad copy and it runs in its own campaign.*

**GRAYHAT  |  5/10**    *Bidding bare competitor names to intercept login or support traffic is allowed in many auctions but yields poor Quality Scores and frustrates users.*

**BLACKHAT  |  1/10**    *Trademark DKI headline exploitation inserts a competitor's brand into your headline dynamically; violates trademark policy and invites suspension.*

## **The AI-era nuance: exact no longer fences broad**

An increasingly important subtlety: exact match no longer fences broad as cleanly as teams assume, especially around AI Max and AI Overviews. Ginny Marvin clarified, as reported by Search Engine Land, that exact keywords do not serve ads in AI Overviews and no longer reliably prevent broad-match keywords from triggering there. The practical consequence is that strict keyword-ownership logic matters less than careful negatives, landing-page alignment, and campaign-level goal separation.

# **Part VII. AI Max for Search and the 2026 Automation Layer**

AI Max for Search is the most important new Search control surface, and the right posture in 2026 is cautious optimism, not blind adoption.

## **What it is and how it behaves**

AI Max is a one-click feature suite layered onto existing Search campaigns, not a standalone campaign type. It turns on three things: search-term matching (broad and keywordless expansion), text customization (Gemini-generated headlines and descriptions assembled to the query and landing page), and Final URL expansion (sending users to the most relevant subpage). It adds brand controls, URL inclusions and exclusions, location-of-interest targeting, and text guidelines. It is also the destination for Dynamic Search Ads: DSA migration into AI Max was extended to February 2027, while Automatically Created Assets and campaign-level broad match begin auto-upgrading in September 2026\.

## **The control levers**

* **Brand settings.** Inclusions define which brands you should appear with; exclusions prevent serving alongside specific competitors or sensitive topics.

* **Text guidelines.** Define messaging rules, exclude promotional terms, and block up to 25 words globally to constrain the generative copy engine, critical for compliance-sensitive categories.

* **URL inclusions and exclusions.** Restrict expansion to verified landing pages so paid traffic never lands on privacy policies, utility pages, or internal blogs.

Sam Clarke of Crossmedia reports that disabling dynamic ad copy and landing-page selection while keeping query expansion produced good results; query expansion itself cannot be turned off.

## **Google's claims versus independent evidence**

Google markets AI Max as delivering, depending on the framing, roughly 7 percent more conversions in non-retail tests, 14 percent, or 27 percent more conversions at similar CPA or ROAS. Treat all of these as marketing. The independent picture is more sobering and more useful:

| Source and test | Finding | Source type |
| :---- | :---- | :---- |

**BLACKHAT  |  2/10**    *Using AI Max URL settings to show crawlers compliant pages while sending searchers elsewhere is dynamic-sitelink cloaking.*

# **Part VIII. Creative Strategy, Assets, and Copywriting**

As bidding and keyword control automate, creative becomes the primary lever of differentiation, and on browse surfaces it doubles as the targeting mechanism.

## **Responsive Search Ad construction**

RSAs work best when the 15 headlines and 4 descriptions give the assembly engine genuinely distinct angles rather than near-duplicates. A practical matrix: headlines 1 to 5 carry core offer and benefit claims; 6 to 10 carry pain-point hooks and emotional triggers; 11 to 15 carry authority proof and risk reversals; the four descriptions pair detailed benefits with urgency CTAs. Pin sparingly, only critical offers or compliance statements to position 1, since pinning everything kills testing. Ad strength is a weak signal; do not sacrifice strong copy to chase an Excellent rating, and use asset-level reporting to prune.

**WHITEHAT  |  10/10**    *Multi-angle RSA testing is standard practice within Google's native creative framework.*

## **YouTube creative for info products**

Aleric Heck of AdOutreach, who states he has spent over $7.7 million on his own YouTube ads, reports that six of his top ten YouTube ads use a hybrid AI structure: an 8-second AI-generated hook that then cuts to a real person. Fully AI ads were his worst performers, because AI can capture attention but does not build trust. The sweet spot is 2 to 3 minutes, built on a hook, value, CTA structure, where the opening hook functions less as copywriting and more as an audience-selection filter: who stays past the hook teaches Google what a good viewer looks like. Heck also recommends turning off connected-TV screens for conversion campaigns.

The micro-VSL (a 30 to 90 second compressed video sales letter) reportedly drives 3 to 5x ROAS on paid social, often using AI avatars and voice clones to mass-produce variations, killing the bottom 70 percent after 48 to 72 hours.

## **Creative for SaaS**

SaaS video runs a problem, agitate, demo, validate arc: a friction hook in the first 5 seconds (why is pipeline forecasting so slow), a fast visual product demo through about 20 seconds, then ROI validation and a free-trial or demo CTA. The creative should show the actual interface in motion.

**WHITEHAT  |  10/10**    *Demo-first SaaS explainers demonstrate real product utility and aid decision-making.*

**GRAYHAT  |  8/10**    *Simulated interface animations are common but must represent real capabilities and avoid overpromising.*

**BLACKHAT  |  1/10**    *Deepfake testimonial voiceovers or AI-faked endorsements violate deceptive-content policy and invite bans and legal action.*

# **Part IX. YouTube, Demand Gen, and the Browse Surfaces**

Demand Gen is no longer a side format. It now spans YouTube, Shorts, Discover, Gmail, Maps, and the Google Display Network, and Google began migrating legacy Display campaigns into Demand Gen starting June 2026\. Sophisticated teams treat it as Google's social-like browse engine, not as Display with better branding.

## **The biggest mistake: optimizing browse like Search**

Search Engine Land's SMX coverage is blunt that Demand Gen fails when teams bring search-first habits into a visual-first environment, and Jyll Saskin Gales lands in the same place: creative and landing-page quality matter more than bid tinkering, and direct last-click conversion expectations are often unrealistic because the channel shapes demand before capture. Bidding should start on Maximize Clicks for audience building, moving to Maximize Conversions with tCPA once tracking is solid.

## **The public lead-gen examples that prove the point**

* **University enrollment.** Distinct creative for students versus parents, optimization toward stronger video-view signals, and pruning weak creative cut CPA from $50 to $60 down to $21.

* **Managed IT and cybersecurity.** A quiz instead of an immediate demo CTA produced $10.24 MQLs and converted 40 percent of quiz completers to SQLs, with a 20 percent lift in total SQLs.

* **Home services (Austin Lindquist, via Jyll's podcast).** Leaving optimized targeting and Display on created significant spam; turning them off cut conversions from 65 to 30, but the remaining leads were materially better. One of the clearest public examples of the tradeoff between reported volume and real quality.

## **Custom intent and first-party signals**

Demand Gen's edge is custom search-intent signals: upload competitor brands or high-intent generic queries and the system targets users who recently searched them. It also supports small Customer Match lists, with the active list minimum reduced to 100 users for EEA and UK in January 2026, letting niche SaaS and info-product advertisers build lookalike-style models from small qualified seed lists. Judge these surfaces on full-funnel contribution (branded-search lift, new-customer acquisition), not last-click ROAS, since view-through credit is easy to overstate.

**WHITEHAT  |  10/10**    *Treating Demand Gen like paid social with creative-led audience selection and lower-friction offers is the correct model.*

**WHITEHAT  |  9/10**    *Search-history custom-intent targeting and role-split creative match offers to audience temperature.*

**GRAYHAT  |  4/10**    *Leaving legacy open-display prospecting on with minimal guardrails produces volume and spam, especially in info-product lead forms.*

## **Shopping and App: mostly out of scope, occasionally dangerous**

Shopping is largely irrelevant and sometimes actively risky for digital goods. Merchant Center policy explicitly disallows services, streaming content, online gaming currency, ebooks and digital books, and many non-tangible goods from standard Shopping, and Google's newer agentic-checkout (UCP) documentation excludes digital goods, services, virtual items, rentals, and most subscriptions from checkout. The narrow exception relevant here is a physical tripwire book. App campaigns matter only when a SaaS product has a real app loop; optimizing to installs is far too shallow for subscription SaaS, where the better move is to optimize toward trial start, activation, or purchase and then graduate to action value or tROAS once signal quality is strong.

**GRAYHAT  |  2/10**    *Running Shopping for courses, SaaS, ebooks, or service offers carries high policy friction and possible Merchant Center fallout; physical tripwire books are the exception.*

**GRAYHAT  |  3/10**    *Optimizing App campaigns only to installs for subscription SaaS scales low-value users and weak retention cohorts.*

# **Part X. The Info-Product Playbook and Compliance Navigation**

The best 2026 info-product accounts are not course campaigns or webinar campaigns. They are temperature systems.

## **Funnels and temperature**

Search captures already-formed intent around outcomes, mechanisms, alternatives, and brand. Demand Gen and YouTube create or intensify demand for category-aware but not yet action-ready users. Remarketing carries the hard-sell assets: testimonials, urgency, objection handling, short VSLs, application breakdowns, and offer-stack reveals. Operators who push cold traffic straight to a long application or checkout without a bridge asset report weak quality or policy trouble. The common funnel shapes:

* **Webinar or challenge funnel.** Ads drive to a free or low-cost event, coordinated with custom-intent signals for training-seekers.

* **Paid community trial (Skool or Circle).** A free trial or $1-per-day entry into a structured community builds recurring subscriptions.

* **Tripwire or book funnel.** A low-ticket physical book or template bundle ($7 to $27) captures buyers, followed by upsells.

## **Bid to a bridge, then graduate**

The field-tested pattern is to let Google optimize to a mid-depth event first, then graduate to harder outcomes: bid to webinar registrations first, then import show-up rate, application approval, booked call, or purchase value later. The repeated community warning: if you make all those soft conversions primary at once, you teach the system to find cheap clickers rather than buyers. Import approval-stage or booked-call outcomes, not just applications, so application funnels do not reward unqualified bookers.

**WHITEHAT  |  9/10**    *Bidding to a bridge conversion then graduating to downstream value gives Smart Bidding volume without anchoring on shallow events.*

**WHITEHAT  |  10/10**    *Importing approval-stage or booked-call outcomes prevents application funnels from rewarding unqualified leads.*

**GRAYHAT  |  6/10**    *Evergreen deadline-funnel timers are standard, but the scarcity must match a genuine offer limit to keep consumer trust.*

## **The compliance minefield**

Info products selling money, health, or personal transformation sit in Google's most dangerous policy territory: Misrepresentation, Unreliable Claims, and Unacceptable Business Practices. Courses, coaching, and communities are especially exposed when they imply guaranteed results, imply affiliation with a public figure, or omit critical terms and refund details. Under the verification-first model tightened through late 2025 and January 2026, financial-education and market-forecasting advertisers may be required to hold regulatory authorization before approval. Compliant operators use prominent bold disclaimers (results vary, not guaranteed), verifiable case studies with real data, and compliant checkout (Stripe, PayPal) with accessible refund policies in the footer. They avoid the egregious no-warning buckets entirely.

| The honest reality of education benchmarks Education and info-product accounts often convert far better in-platform than SaaS (WordStream by LocaliQ puts Education and Instruction CVR around 13.14 percent with CTR up about 31.71 percent year over year and CPC down about 22.79 percent). But that can mislead: many info-product funnels optimize to webinar registrations, VSL views, or low-friction tripwires, so judged-by-Google metrics flatter them. The strongest named webinar case is HawkSEM with TimeWarp Trading, a trading-education funnel reporting a 471 percent ROAS increase, 9.09 percent ad CTR, 30.22 percent conversion rate, and CPA around 5 percent of sale (vendor-published). High-ticket coaching webinars typically convert 5 to 10 percent of registrants, with 40 to 50 percent attending live. |
| :---- |

**WHITEHAT  |  10/10**    *Verified case-study funnels with prominent outcome disclaimers and interactive challenge funnels are compliant and sustainable.*

**BLACKHAT  |  1/10**    *Get-rich-quick, guaranteed-income, or absolute transformation claims violate the unreliable-claims policy and trigger immediate bans and consumer harm.*

**BLACKHAT  |  1/10**    *Hiding offer terms, refund conditions, billing cadence, or subscription renewals is a classic suspension and consumer-harm trigger.*

# **Part XI. The SaaS Playbook and Pipeline Value Bidding**

SaaS acquisition differs fundamentally from e-commerce: long cycles, buying committees of six to ten people, and three distinct motions with radically different economics.

## **Three motions, three optimization targets**

* **Self-serve and freemium (PLG).** Capture high-volume ready-to-use queries (category tool, free category software) and optimize to activation events, not mere account creation. Public benchmarks suggest the top of the funnel is the weakest part of SaaS PPC, with paid-search visitor-to-lead rates around 0.7 percent in the First Page Sage dataset, so every point of post-click activation matters more than marginal CTR.

* **Free trial.** Optimize toward trial-start then activation, with deep-linking to a real onboarding step rather than a generic login.

* **Demo-led (sales-assisted).** Target enterprise and solution-aware queries (category platform for enterprise, implementation service) and optimize to SQL or closed-won via offline imports.

## **Bid to pipeline truth, not form-fill vanity**

The feedback loop of doom: without CRM data, PMax and Smart Bidding optimize to the cheapest form fills, which are rarely qualified. The cure is to bid to the right event. If a form-fill CPA is $150 and MQL-to-SQL is 15 percent, the true cost per SQL is about $1,000. Set tCPA at cost per SQL, not cost per form fill, and migrate from tCPA toward value-based tROAS tied to CRM stages so the engine prioritizes high-value enterprise deals over low-fit signups.

## **Funnel-stage leakage benchmarks**

Two datasets, presented side by side because they disagree and both are vendor-sourced. Use them as directional only:

| Stage transition | Involve Digital style range | First Page Sage dataset |
| :---- | :---- | :---- |
| Visitor to lead | Landing-page CVR 3 to 5 percent | 0.7 percent |
| Lead to MQL | 40 to 60 percent | 36 percent |
| MQL to SQL | 13 to 22 percent | 26 percent |
| SQL to opportunity | 50 to 60 percent | 38 percent |
| Opportunity to close | 16 to 30 percent | 35 percent |
| Click to customer | 0.5 to 1.5 percent | Implied low single digits |

Supporting economics from the same vendor research: blended first-touch ROAS around 78 percent (below breakeven, which is exactly why the CRM loop matters), brand ROAS near 1,200 percent on about 7 percent of budget, an LTV to CAC target of 3 to 1 (top quartile 5 to 1 or better), median CAC payback around 23 months, and an average B2B SaaS CAC around $728 with average campaign ROI from PPC around 36 percent (First Page Sage).

## **Named SaaS results, labeled by source**

| Account | Result | Source type |
| :---- | :---- | :---- |
| PriceLabs | ROAS 0.7x to 2.5x on about $100k spend | Vendor self-published |
| Trackxi | 4x trial volume at 51 percent lower cost per trial (PMax plus offline imports plus HubSpot Customer Match) | Vendor self-published |

**WHITEHAT  |  10/10**    *Optimizing to activation, SQL, or revenue stage instead of trial or demo alone correlates with LTV and payback.*

**WHITEHAT  |  9/10**    *Separating self-serve, free-trial, and demo-led motions avoids one bid target averaging incompatible journeys.*

**GRAYHAT  |  6/10**    *Marketing a platform as free when core features sit behind a paywall inflates conversion volume and degrades lead quality.*

**BLACKHAT  |  1/10**    *Synthetic account creation or scripted trial signups to inflate conversions skews bidding and invites suspension.*

# **Part XII. Scripts, Automation, and Agentic AI Workflows**

Scripts still matter in 2026 for one revealing reason: operators do not run them because Google Ads lacks automation. They run them because platform automation still leaves blind spots.

## **The named script stack**

* **Mike Rhodes PMax script.** Roughly 3,200 lines, with a free version on GitHub and a paid tier. It splits PMax channel spend, buckets search categories (brand, close-to-brand, non-brand) and products (zombies, zero-conversion, profitable), surfaces placements, and audits assets. Used inside agencies from Dentsu to Publicis and by brands including Thinkific.

* **N-gram analyzers.** From Optmyzr, Adalysis, and community authors (Daniel Gilbert of Brainlabs, Mike Rhodes with Aaron Young) for wasted-spend mining and negative automation.

* **smec tooling.** A Brand Analyzer for cannibalization percentage and a Placement Scanner or Cleaner that excludes junk placements across an MCC by page title.

* **Non-converting search-term alerts.** Nils Rooijmans' script logs offenders to a sheet and emails them; anomaly detectors run hourly.

* **Budget pacing guardrails.** Compare month-to-date spend to target, with the script schedule aligned to the account timezone to avoid spend-calculation mismatches.

**WHITEHAT  |  10/10**    *Scripts for placement audits, n-grams, anomaly detection, and pacing fill blind spots the native UI leaves.*

## **The agentic AI layer, realistically**

The agentic layer is real but immature in public evidence. Frederick Vallaeys of Optmyzr frames the data-wall thesis: exporting data and pasting it into a chat is not automation. MCP connectors, including Optmyzr's and Google's open-source MCP server, give LLMs live account access, and Optmyzr layers business-intelligence guardrails so a hallucinated campaign ID or lookback does not damage the account. Claude Skills can encode standard operating procedures (audit checklists, n-gram mining) as reusable agent playbooks. The strongest validated 2026 use cases are monitoring and QA, not autonomous bidding: search-term mining, negative suggestions, placement-risk scans, creative-gap detection, CRM-mismatch alerts, and asset-brief generation. Ann Stanley's AI sandwich captures the discipline: humans at the top and bottom, AI in the middle.

**WHITEHAT  |  8/10**    *Using LLMs as QA and decision support, with human review before execution, is the strongest current use; full autonomy lacks independent validation.*

**GRAYHAT  |  7/10**    *Autonomous API multi-variant campaign spawning without human review produces low-quality creative at scale.*

# **Part XIII. Policy, Compliance, Reinstatement, and the Anatomy of Cloaking**

Operating in competitive or sensitive niches demands a working knowledge of enforcement and recovery, and an honest map of the blackhat techniques so teams can recognize them, not deploy them.

## **The scale of enforcement**

Google's 2024 Ads Safety Report, released April 2025, states Google suspended 39.2 million advertiser accounts in 2024, more than triple the 12.7 million of 2023, with the majority suspended before any ads served, using more than 50 LLM enhancements covering 97 percent of enforcement actions. Appeals are now roughly 70 percent faster, with 99 percent processed within 24 hours as of November 2025, and Google claims Gemini cut incorrect suspensions by about 80 percent. Faster does not mean a higher approval rate.

## **Trigger categories**

* **Circumventing systems.** Hidden redirects, cloaking, malicious code, tracking templates that fail to match the final URL, sitelinks pointing to different content, multiple-account abuse, or a cluster of disapprovals in a short window. An egregious, no-warning bucket.

* **Misrepresentation and unreliable claims.** Exaggerated performance claims without evidence, hidden subscription costs, missing refund or contact details, and the health, wealth, and transformation claims that are most dangerous for info products.

* **Suspicious payments.** Billing details that do not match the card, or multiple accounts sharing a payment method across regions.

A three-strike system (warning, 3-day, 7-day, permanent) applies outside the egregious buckets.

## **The reinstatement framework**

If suspended, work the problem in order: diagnose via Policy Manager and change logs to find the trigger; remediate by removing flagged copy, fixing billing, and correcting redirects; assemble an evidence pack (incorporation documents matching the payment profile, bank statements matching the card, server logs showing removed scripts); then submit one strong, factual appeal. Fix the website and landing page first, since reinstatement specialists work through 160-plus-step site checklists, and pass identity and business verification before appealing. Repeated weak appeals worsen the case.

| Factual appeal skeleton 1\. Root-cause analysis. State the specific violation and account ID, then explain the technical cause plainly (for example, an outdated dynamic tracking template that broke final-URL consistency) and that it was an error, not an attempt to bypass the system. 2\. Remediation steps. List concrete fixes: corrected tracking templates, verified landing-page accessibility and mobile speed via URL Inspection, and a footer updated with the registered entity and refund policy. 3\. Documentation. Reference the attached evidence pack (articles of incorporation matching the payment name exactly, matching bank statements, and server-log history confirming removal of outdated scripts). |
| :---- |

**WHITEHAT  |  10/10**    *Completing required verification and submitting factual, well-documented appeals is the correct recovery path.*

**GRAYHAT  |  5/10**    *Migrating to new domains to dodge automated flagging can trigger manual review if done excessively.*

## **The anatomy of cloaking (documented, not endorsed)**

In heavily restricted verticals, some advertisers run cloaking tools that split traffic into two destinations: a clean safe page built to satisfy reviewers and crawl bots, and a money page (the aggressive or non-compliant offer) shown to real users. Cloaking engines fingerprint requests using IP matching against Google ranges, browser characteristics, scroll behavior, screen resolution, fonts, and geolocation. By mid-2026 Google's detection deploys behavior-based flagging, machine-learning crawlers that mimic human interaction, and cross-account footprint tracking that links and suspends connected networks. Search Engine Land names cloaking, redirect chains, burner multi-account setups, and coded policy language directly as spammy enforcement-evasion patterns. These are suspension bait and create direct consumer harm; they belong in an atlas only so teams know what they are.

**BLACKHAT  |  1/10**    *Fingerprint or redirect cloaking to bypass review carries severe risk of permanent, often network-wide suspension and harms consumers.*

**BLACKHAT  |  1/10**    *Multi-account recycling, redirect chains, coded-policy language, and burner domains are deceptive and high-risk.*

**GRAYHAT  |  3/10**    *Uploading consent-poor or policy-unclear first-party lists into Customer Match is powerful but now carries much higher risk; Search Engine Land reports misuse can trigger immediate suspension without warning.*

# **Part XIV. Cross-Surface Orchestration and Incrementality**

High-volume programs require coordinated management across surfaces, and measurement that distinguishes what got credit from what actually caused the result.

## **The customer journey map**

Demand Gen, YouTube, Shorts, and Discover build the audience pool at the top. Generic Search and AI Max capture problem-aware queries. PMax and, where eligible, Standard Shopping remarket to warm visitors. Exact-match brand protection secures decision-intent conversions at the bottom. Each surface hands off to the next rather than competing for the same credit.

## **Phased budget allocation**

Budgets should scale in stages as conversion data stabilizes, the inverse of trying to run full-funnel from day one:

* **Phase 1 ($0 to $10k per month).** About 90 percent to bottom-of-funnel Search to capture immediate intent and establish baselines.

* **Phase 2 ($10k to $30k per month).** Roughly 70 percent Search and 30 percent PMax, adding Customer Match lists and, for physical goods, Standard Shopping.

* **Phase 3 ($30k plus per month).** Layer in Demand Gen and YouTube at about 15 to 20 percent for demand creation.

## **Incrementality over attribution**

Attribution dashboards tell you who got credit; incrementality tells you what advertising actually caused. That distinction matters most in brand Search, video, and Demand Gen, where assisted lift is large and last-click is wildly misleading. Use Google's geo-based Conversion Lift, geo holdouts, matched-market tests, and PMax and Demand Gen experiments rather than trusting blended last-click. Track macro metrics, the Media Efficiency Ratio (MER) and Marketing Mix Modeling (MMM), to bypass single-platform cookie gaps and estimate true channel contribution.

**WHITEHAT  |  10/10**    *Geo holdouts and Conversion Lift on brand Search, Demand Gen, and YouTube separate increment from mere attribution.*

**WHITEHAT  |  10/10**    *MMM and MER measure incremental contribution while sidestepping cookie-tracking limits.*

**GRAYHAT  |  7/10**    *Consent-aligned cross-domain retargeting via customer lists must comply with local privacy and consent frameworks.*

# **Part XV. Contrarian Plays and Rep-Defiance Protocols**

Google account representatives are incentivized toward platform spend, not advertiser efficiency. The contrarian stance sophisticated operators take is not anti-automation; it is selective automation: embrace Smart Bidding, AI Max, and browse surfaces only after building signal hygiene, and keep manual controls alive longer than reps prefer when data integrity is not yet there.

## **The core divergences**

| Google rep advice | What experienced operators do |
| :---- | :---- |
| Enable auto-apply recommendations | Turn auto-apply off; recommendations often opt you into broad match and expansion that lift CPA |
| Let PMax handle branded search | Apply account-level brand exclusions so PMax chases new customers, not cheap brand demand |
| Build narrow demographic audiences on YouTube | Test open targeting in high-data accounts so creative self-segments at lower cost |
| Do not cap Smart Bidding | Run portfolio tCPA with max-bid caps to avoid overpaying outlier auctions |
| Graduate from Standard Shopping to PMax | Run feed-only PMax from day one when product intent is strong (SavvyRevenue) |
| Trust optimization score | Ignore it as a performance metric; a 50-percent-growth account hitting tROAS scored just 72.8 percent |

Community sentiment on reps is consistent: use them for implementation details and product availability, not for channel mix or budget recommendations in your market.

**WHITEHAT  |  9/10**    *Disabling auto-apply, aggressive brand negativing, open-targeting video tests, and bid-capped Smart Bidding are sustainable contrarian controls.*

**WHITEHAT  |  8/10**    *Ignoring Google reps on bidding and channel advice unless your CRM data agrees is a sound operator hedge.*

**GRAYHAT  |  7/10**    *Deliberate under-bidding and Max-Clicks or capped tactics to starve premature Smart Bidding is useful in low-volume accounts and exploits the learning system.*

# **Part XVII. The Implementation Playbook**

A staged sequence. Do not skip ahead; each stage is the prerequisite for the next. The order is deliberate: measurement first, because every clever tactic downstream depends on clean signal.

## **Stage 1\. Foundation (Weeks 1 to 4\)**

1. **Fix measurement first.** Enhanced Conversions (web and Leads), Advanced Consent Mode v2, and GCLID capture into the CRM. Highest-ROI work in the Atlas. Advance only when Enhanced Conversions match rate clears 40 percent and modeling is active.

2. **Isolate brand.** Build a dedicated brand Search campaign and apply Brand Exclusions to every non-brand and PMax campaign. Verify branded share in PMax search-term insights falls toward zero.

3. **Consolidate structure.** Move to thematic Hagakure Search, kill SKAGs, and build the SaaS and info negative lists (free, jobs, login, tutorial, salary, careers).

## **Stage 2\. Signal and bidding (Weeks 4 to 12\)**

1. **Climb the bid ladder.** Do not jump to tROAS without value data. For SaaS, bid to SQL or closed-won via offline imports, not form fills. For info products, optimize to purchase or webinar-attended.

2. **Install the script stack.** Mike Rhodes PMax script plus an n-gram analyzer; review search terms weekly, daily on high spend.

3. **Stabilize low-volume accounts.** For high-ticket or low-volume accounts, consolidate and assign micro-conversion values rather than starving the algorithm.

## **Stage 3\. Surface expansion and testing (Month 3 and beyond)**

1. **Add demand creation.** Bring on YouTube and Demand Gen (info products lean here heavily). Use tCPA seeding around $8 with 15 percent increments, hybrid AI hooks, 2 to 3 minute creative, and custom-intent audiences.

2. **Run AI Max as a contained experiment.** One well-structured campaign, all three features on, full guardrails (brand restrictions, URL exclusions, text guidelines). Evaluate at the account level for true incrementality. Kill it if account-level CPL or CAC worsens.

3. **Prove incrementality before scaling.** Use PMax and Demand Gen experiments and geo holdouts to confirm causal lift before pouring in budget.

## **The thresholds that change the plan**

| Under 30 conversions per month per campaign: stay on Maximize Conversions, consolidate, avoid tROAS and aggressive broad match. Under about $5,000 per month total, consider staying off Smart Bidding entirely. AI Max account-level CPL up or conversions down after 2 to 4 weeks: revert to broad match plus Smart Bidding. The 84 percent neutral-or-negative base rate makes default skepticism rational. PMax branded share above 10 percent: tighten brand exclusions immediately. Suspension-risk signals (income claims, before-and-after, sensitive categories): remediate creative and landing pages before scaling, and never run multiple accounts to evade. |

# **Appendix B. Caveats and Evidence Quality**

This Atlas is strongest on Search, Performance Max, Demand Gen, AI Max, signal infrastructure, policy, and SaaS and info-product funnel design. It is weaker where the public record is weaker.

* **Source quality is uneven.** Many vivid figures (AdOutreach CPLs and ROAS, GrowthSpree SQL reductions and named-client ROAS, Teachery, PriceLabs, Trackxi, Rocketlane) are vendor or self-published marketing, often anonymous testimonials. Treated as directional, not audited. The most neutral benchmarks are WordStream by LocaliQ and Optmyzr's studies; the strongest named info-product case (HawkSEM with TimeWarp Trading) is still vendor-published.

* **The Moran feeder headline numbers are e-commerce, not lead gen.** The \+108 percent, \+127 percent, and 1,000-percent-ROAS results are Shopping plus PMax cases. The lead-gen spam-reduction benefit is described qualitatively, and one circulating lead-gen figure is loosely sourced.

* **AI Max and PMax are moving targets.** Controls and auto-upgrade timelines (DSA into AI Max by February 2027; Automatically Created Assets and broad match from September 2026\) change frequently. Verify in-account before relying on any lever. The independent datasets (Brainlabs 23 tests, Smarter Ecommerce 250 plus campaigns, the Dekker poll, PPC Live) consistently show AI Max underdelivering versus Google's marketing.

* **Google's own performance claims are marketing.** The 7, 14, and 27 percent AI Max figures, the 27-percent Smart Bidding Exploration figure, the 33-percent Demand Gen feeds figure, and the up-to-70-percent Consent Mode recovery figure are Google-published. Independent testing shows smaller, conditional, or negative results.

* **Ethical scores are this Atlas's judgment.** They reflect policy compliance and consumer-harm risk on a whitehat to blackhat spectrum and are not Google's official classifications. Blackhat tactics are documented for recognition and explicitly flagged; do not deploy them.

* **Regional and vertical variance is large.** A high CPA can be profitable at high LTV and vice versa. Benchmarks calibrate; they do not target.

* **Thinnest public evidence.** Journey-aware bidding, AI Max for Shopping, fully agentic account management, independent app-campaign SaaS case studies, and independent info-product CPC, CPL, and ROAS benchmarks. Treat these as test programs, not doctrine.
