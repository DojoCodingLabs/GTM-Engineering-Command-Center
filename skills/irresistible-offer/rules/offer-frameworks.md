# Offer Frameworks: Stacking, Risk Reversal, Urgency & Scarcity

## Offer Stacking

An irresistible offer is never just one thing. You stack components so the perceived value dramatically exceeds the price. Each layer attacks a different purchase objection.

### The 5-Layer Stack

**Layer 1: Core Product**
The main deliverable. Define it with extreme specificity.
- Bad: "Online course"
- Good: "12-week live cohort program with 48 hours of instruction across 24 sessions"

**Layer 2: Speed Bonus**
Compresses time to result. Attacks the "this will take forever" objection.
- Templates, swipe files, copy-paste frameworks
- Pre-built configurations or starter kits
- Fast-start guides (get first result in 24 hours)
- Example: "47 production-ready project templates so you skip weeks of setup"

**Layer 3: Certainty Bonus**
Increases perceived likelihood of success. Attacks the "what if it doesn't work for me" objection.
- Community access (others doing it = social proof you can too)
- Mentorship or office hours (expert guidance when stuck)
- Case studies and playbooks from people similar to the buyer
- Example: "Weekly group coaching calls so you're never stuck for more than 7 days"

**Layer 4: Ease Bonus**
Reduces effort required. Attacks the "this seems like a lot of work" objection.
- Done-for-you services (we set it up for you)
- Checklists and SOPs (just follow the steps)
- Automation tools or scripts
- Example: "Personal onboarding call where we configure your account together"

**Layer 5: Risk Reversal**
Removes the downside of buying. Attacks the "what if I waste my money" objection.
- See detailed section below.

### Value Anchoring

Always show the individual value of each component before revealing the bundled price:

```
Core program:          $2,997 value
+ 47 templates:          $497 value
+ Weekly coaching:     $1,997 value
+ Community access:      $297 value
+ 1-on-1 onboarding:    $497 value
─────────────────────────────────
Total value:           $6,285

Your investment today:   $997
```

The gap between stacked value and actual price creates the "irresistible" feeling.

### Pricing Psychology for LATAM

- Always show monthly payment option first: "$83/mes" feels lighter than "$997"
- Show savings for annual: "Ahorra $199 con el plan anual"
- Use odd pricing: $997 > $1,000 (perceived as "under a thousand")
- When possible, compare to daily costs: "Menos que un cafe al dia"

## Risk Reversal

Risk reversal moves the risk from the buyer to the seller. The stronger your guarantee, the higher your conversion rate.

### Guarantee Hierarchy (weakest to strongest)

1. **No guarantee** -- Buyer assumes all risk. Lowest conversion.
2. **Satisfaction guarantee** -- "If you're not satisfied, contact us." Vague, weak.
3. **Money-back guarantee** -- "30-day full refund, no questions asked." Standard.
4. **Results guarantee** -- "If you don't get [specific result], full refund." Strong.
5. **Better-than-money-back** -- "If you don't get [result], full refund + I'll pay you $500." Nuclear option.

### Crafting a Results Guarantee

Template: "If you [do the work / complete the program / use the product for X days] and don't [achieve specific measurable result], we'll [refund / extend / compensate]."

Example: "Complete all 12 modules and submit your capstone project. If you don't land at least 3 job interviews within 90 days of graduation, we'll refund 100% of your tuition and give you 6 additional months of coaching free."

### Why Strong Guarantees Work

- Refund rates rarely exceed 5-10% even with aggressive guarantees
- The conversion lift from the guarantee (often 30-50%) far exceeds refund losses
- People who request refunds weren't going to become long-term customers anyway
- A guarantee signals confidence in your product

### Guarantee Copy Patterns

- "Garantia total de 30 dias. Si no te funciona, te devolvemos cada centavo."
- "Zero risk. Try it for 14 days. If it's not for you, one email gets your money back."
- "We're so confident you'll love this that we guarantee [result] or your money back."

## Urgency (Time-Based)

Urgency creates a deadline for action. It must be real -- fake urgency destroys trust.

### Legitimate Urgency Types

**Cohort-based**: "The next cohort starts March 15. Registration closes March 10."
- Real: cohorts have actual start dates
- Show how many cohorts per year (scarcity amplifier)

**Event-based**: "Early bird pricing ends when we hit 100 registrations."
- Real: events have capacity limits
- Show counter: "73/100 spots filled"

**Promotional**: "Launch price: $497 (regular price $997 starting April 1)."
- Real: introductory pricing is standard practice
- Must actually raise the price on the stated date

**Seasonal**: "Enroll before Q2 so you can implement before your busy season."
- Real: business cycles create natural deadlines
- Connect urgency to the buyer's timeline, not yours

### Urgency Copy Patterns

- "Doors close [date] at midnight. No extensions."
- "This price is only available during launch week."
- "We only run this program twice a year. Next cohort isn't until [month]."
- "Quedan solo [X] lugares disponibles para esta cohorte."

## Scarcity (Quantity-Based)

Scarcity limits the supply. Like urgency, it must be real.

### Legitimate Scarcity Types

**Capacity scarcity**: "Limited to 30 students per cohort for quality mentorship."
- Real: instructor bandwidth is finite
- Justify the limit: explain WHY it's limited

**Access scarcity**: "Beta access for first 100 users only."
- Real: beta programs have legitimate capacity constraints
- Show the counter: "67 of 100 beta spots claimed"

**Bonus scarcity**: "First 50 buyers get a free 1-on-1 strategy call."
- Real: your time for calls is limited
- The core offer stays available; the bonus is what's scarce

### Scarcity Copy Patterns

- "Solo 30 lugares por cohorte. Cuando se llena, se llena."
- "This bonus is limited to the first 50 enrollees."
- "We intentionally keep cohorts small. Join the waitlist for the next opening."

### Anti-Patterns (Never Do)

- Fake countdown timers that reset on page refresh
- "Only 3 left!" when there's unlimited digital inventory
- Urgency on an evergreen product with no actual deadline
- Running "limited time" promotions that never end
