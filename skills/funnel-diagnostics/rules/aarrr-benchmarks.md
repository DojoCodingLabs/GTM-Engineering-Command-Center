# AARRR Benchmarks -- Industry Benchmarks by Vertical

## How to Use These Benchmarks

Benchmarks provide the "expected" value against which you compare your "actual" value. A conversion rate is only "bad" relative to what's achievable for your type of product.

**Rules for benchmark comparison:**
1. Compare to the closest matching vertical (not the best-performing one)
2. Use the "Good" column as your target, not "Elite" (elite requires exceptional execution)
3. Benchmarks assume the metric has sufficient sample size (30+ data points minimum)
4. Adjust benchmarks +/- 20% for geo (LATAM typically 10-20% lower than US/EU for paid conversion)

## Interpreting the Benchmarks

### The Gap Analysis Formula

```
Gap = Benchmark (Good) - Actual
Revenue Impact = Gap x Monthly Volume x Revenue Per Conversion

Example:
  Metric: Trial-to-paid conversion
  Actual: 8%
  Benchmark (Good for SaaS): 15%
  Gap: 7 percentage points
  Monthly trials: 500
  Revenue per paid user: $30/month MRR

  Revenue Impact = 0.07 x 500 x $30 = $1,050/month MRR being left on table
  Annual impact: $12,600 ARR
```

### Adjustment Factors

```
Geo adjustment (multiply benchmark by factor):
  US/Canada: 1.0x (benchmarks are calibrated to US)
  Western Europe: 0.9x
  LATAM: 0.7-0.85x
  Southeast Asia: 0.6-0.8x
  India: 0.5-0.7x

Price point adjustment:
  < $10/month: +20% conversion benchmark (lower barrier)
  $10-50/month: 1.0x (standard)
  $50-200/month: -20% conversion benchmark
  > $200/month: -40% conversion benchmark

Channel adjustment:
  Organic (SEO, referral): +30% conversion benchmark (higher intent)
  Paid (Meta, Google): 1.0x (standard)
  Social (organic): -30% conversion benchmark (lower intent)
```

### When Benchmarks Don't Apply

```
Situations where benchmarks are misleading:
  1. Brand new category (no comparable product)
  2. First 100 customers (too small for statistical reliability)
  3. Enterprise sales (cycles are 60-180 days, not 14-day trials)
  4. Marketplace with chicken-and-egg problem (supply side affects demand metrics)
  5. Heavily regulated industry (compliance adds friction that benchmarks don't account for)

In these cases, use your own historical data as the benchmark.
Week-over-week improvement > absolute benchmark comparison.
```
